# What Is Credit Risk?

Siirretty etusivulta:

Assessing objectively and realistically the financial state of a company can be a difficult challenge. Acquiring the relevant information for having a thorough understanding of the risks of new opportunities or existing contracts can be tedious. Whether the company is a soon-to-be business partner or an older collaborator, it is vital for you to know who you are doing business with. Not only that, you must know your own weaknesses and possible downfalls accurately beforehand. Recognizing these risks helps you to prevent losing valuable resources and signing detrimental deals.

What is credit risk?

Credit risk is the probable risk of the borrower not being able to repay a loan or make the required contractual payments. The risk is associated with the borrower not being able to pay the owed principles and interests, disrupting the cashflows of the lender and resulting in increased collections costs.

The borrower’s overall ability to repay their obligations determines the credit risk. Interest rate is the reward or the compensation for the assumed risk and the higher the risk, the higher the borrowing costs. A customer with a lower credit risk naturally receives lower costs, since it is more likely the credit will be repaid than by someone who has a greater credit risk.

Why is credit risk important?

Determining accurate risks for a credit loan or a contract with a new business is important in numerous ways:

- Knowing the risks will help to set the right interest rates and costs for credit obligations.
- Gives a more precise indication whether the customer will manage their payments in time.
- Mitigates losses if the outcome is not desirable and the borrower has problems with payments.
- The number of credit suppliers that will extend you is bigger.

How is credit risk calculated?

There are several ways to assess credit risk. These are all just estimates, however, since there is not a single correct way to determine this. Information from financial statements, news and other sources provide a vast selection of variables, ratios and knowledge that can be used to assess the risks precisely. The most important borrower-specific factors are usually related to debt-to-income ratios, liquidity, profitability, size measures and expenses. Market-wide criteria are also considered that include historical default trends and a variety of macroeconomic factors.

Variables are also compared between businesses that defaulted. Identifying the common factors that these companies had in common gives a better understanding what affects the probability of a company defaulting.
